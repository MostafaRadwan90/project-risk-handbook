Section A: Short Answer Questions
Question 1 (5 Marks)
Define "Risk Appetite" and explain how it differs from "Risk Tolerance."

Model Answer:

Risk Appetite: Risk appetite is the degree of uncertainty an entity (organization or individual) is willing to take on in anticipation of a reward. It's a high-level, strategic guideline that reflects the organization's general attitude toward risk-taking to achieve its objectives.

Difference from Risk Tolerance:

  • Risk Appetite is a broader, strategic concept about the *amount* and *type* of risk an organization is willing to pursue or retain.
  • Risk Tolerance is more specific and operational. It refers to the specified range of acceptable variation or deviation an entity is willing to accept around specific objectives (e.g., a +/- 10% tolerance on budget). Tolerance operates within the boundaries set by the overall risk appetite.

Rationale: Based on Handbook Chapter 1, Slides 7 & 8.

Question 2 (5 Marks)
List five common components of a Pre-tender Health and Safety Plan.

Model Answer:

Five common components of a Pre-tender Health and Safety Plan include:

  1. General description and location of the work.
  2. Existing on-site conditions (e.g., ground conditions, existing utilities, known hazards).
  3. Hazards from existing structures (especially for demolition or refurbishment).
  4. The design (highlighting inherent H&S considerations or challenges).
  5. Site rules and procedures (client-specific H&S requirements contractors must adhere to).

Rationale: Based on Handbook Chapter 10, Slide 81.

Question 3 (5 Marks)
What is a Risk Breakdown Structure (RBS) and why is it useful in project risk management?

Model Answer:

Risk Breakdown Structure (RBS): The RBS is a hierarchical structure of potential risk sources or categories of risk for a project. It provides a framework for systematically identifying risks by breaking down the project into various areas where risks might originate.

Usefulness:

  • Comprehensive Identification: Helps ensure a more thorough and systematic process of risk identification by prompting consideration of various risk categories.
  • Organization: Assists in categorizing and organizing identified risks in the risk register.
  • Communication: Provides a common framework for discussing and understanding risks among stakeholders.
  • Checklist Basis: The lowest levels of an RBS can be used as a starting point for risk checklists.
  • Focus: Helps to focus risk identification efforts on specific areas of the project or types of risk.

Rationale: Based on Handbook Chapter 3, Slide 16.

Section B: Multiple Choice Questions
4. A project manager decides to change the project design to use a well-known technology instead of a new, unproven one to avoid potential technical failures. This is an example of which risk response strategy?
  • A. Mitigate
  • B. Transfer
  • C. Avoid
  • D. Accept

Correct Answer: C

Rationale: Changing the plan to eliminate the threat (unproven technology) entirely is risk avoidance. (Handbook Chapter 7, Slide 56)

5. The process of numerically analyzing the combined effect of identified individual project risks on overall project objectives is known as:
  • A. Perform Qualitative Risk Analysis
  • B. Identify Risks
  • C. Perform Quantitative Risk Analysis
  • D. Plan Risk Responses

Correct Answer: C

Rationale: This is the definition of Perform Quantitative Risk Analysis. (Handbook Chapter 6, Introduction)

6. Which of the following is typically an OUTPUT of the Plan Risk Management process?
  • A. Risk Register
  • B. Risk Management Plan
  • C. Prioritized list of risks
  • D. Risk Response Plan

Correct Answer: B

Rationale: The primary output of Plan Risk Management is the Risk Management Plan. (Handbook Chapter 2, Slide 13)

7. In the Loss Causation Model, "Substandard Acts or Practices" are considered:
  • A. Basic Causes
  • B. Immediate Causes
  • C. Lack of Control
  • D. Loss

Correct Answer: B

Rationale: Substandard Acts (unsafe acts) and Substandard Conditions (unsafe conditions) are categorized as Immediate Causes in the Loss Causation Model. (Handbook Chapter 15, Slide 165)

8. A construction company partners with a specialized engineering firm to implement an innovative tunneling technique that could significantly reduce project duration, sharing the potential time savings. This is an example of which strategy for positive risks?
  • A. Exploit
  • B. Enhance
  • C. Share
  • D. Accept

Correct Answer: C

Rationale: Allocating ownership of an opportunity to a third party who is best able to capture it for the benefit of the project, and sharing the benefits, is the Share strategy. (Handbook Chapter 8, Slide 66)

9. The Total Recordable Incident Rate (TRIR) is an example of a:
  • A. Leading Indicator
  • B. Lagging Indicator
  • C. Predictive Indicator
  • D. Proactive Measure

Correct Answer: B

Rationale: TRIR tracks incidents that have already occurred, making it a reactive or lagging indicator. (Handbook Chapter 13, Slide 113)

10. If a risk event has a 25% chance of occurring, and the consequence is a cost of $80,000, what is the Expected Monetary Value (EMV)?
  • A. $20,000
  • B. -$20,000
  • C. $80,000
  • D. -$60,000

Correct Answer: B

Rationale: EMV = Probability * Impact. For a threat (cost), the impact is negative. EMV = 0.25 * (-$80,000) = -$20,000. (Handbook Chapter 6)
