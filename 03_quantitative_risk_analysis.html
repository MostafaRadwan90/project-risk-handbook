Chapter 6: Perform Quantitative Risk Analysis

Definition and Purpose

Perform Quantitative Risk Analysis is the process of numerically analyzing the effect of identified individual project risks (usually those prioritized by qualitative analysis) on overall project objectives. It produces quantitative risk information to support decision-making in order to reduce project uncertainty.

The key goals are to:

  • Assign a numerical value to the impact of each risk.
  • Quantify the possible outcomes for the project and their probabilities.
  • Assess the probability of achieving specific project objectives (e.g., cost and schedule targets).
  • Identify the most influential risks on the project and their relative impact.
  • Determine how much contingency reserve (time or money) might be needed.

(Source: Slides: Project Risk Management - Part 2 (Qualitative/Quantitative), Slide 7; Slides: Project Risk Management - Part 1, Slide 42)

When to Perform Quantitative Analysis

Quantitative analysis is not always performed on all projects or for all risks. It is most likely to be used when:

  • Projects are large, complex, or strategically important.
  • There is a need for more robust, numerical justification for risk responses or contingency reserves.
  • Stakeholders require a more detailed understanding of overall project risk exposure.
  • High-priority risks identified through qualitative analysis need further investigation.

6.1 Qualitative vs. Quantitative Risk Analysis

Understanding the distinctions and similarities between these two analysis types is crucial.

Feature Qualitative Risk Analysis Quantitative Risk Analysis
Nature of Evaluation Subjective evaluation Objective and numerical evaluation
Result Format Descriptive (e.g., High, Medium, Low risk) Numerical (e.g., $10,000 impact, 70% probability of success)
Application Typically required for all identified risks Not required for all risks; often applied to high-priority risks
Precision Less precise analysis More precise analysis
Ease of Performance Easier to perform More difficult to perform; may require specialized tools/skills
Primary Purpose Prioritize risks for further action or monitoring Numerically analyze the effect of risks on project objectives

Similarities: Both aim to understand and manage risks, use the Risk Register as input, assess probability and impact (though differently), support decision-making, can be iterative, and are part of the overall risk management framework.

6.2 Critical Success Factors for Quantitative Analysis

  • Prior risk identification and qualitative analysis (to focus efforts).
  • An appropriate project model (e.g., schedule, cost estimate).
  • Commitment to collecting high-quality risk data.
  • Unbiased data.
  • Overall project risk derived from individual risks.
  • Consideration of interrelationships between risks.

(Source: Slides: Project Risk Management - Part 2 (Qualitative/Quantitative), Slide 10)

6.4 Key Tools & Techniques Explained

6.4.1 Data Gathering and Representation

Quantitative analysis often requires representing uncertainty using probability distributions. Common distributions include:

  • Triangular Distribution: Uses three estimates: Optimistic (O), Most Likely (M), Pessimistic (P). Often used when data is limited.
  • Beta Distribution: Also uses O, M, P estimates but allows for more skewed distributions. Commonly used in PERT analysis.
  • Normal Distribution (Gaussian): Bell-shaped curve, defined by mean and standard deviation. Assumes data is symmetrically distributed around the mean.
  • Uniform Distribution: All values within a range are equally likely. Used when there's no obvious most likely value.
  • Lognormal Distribution: Skewed distribution often used for variables that cannot be negative, like durations or costs.

Data for these distributions can be gathered through expert interviews, historical data, or commercial databases.

6.4.2 Expected Monetary Value (EMV) Analysis

EMV is a statistical concept that calculates the average outcome when the future includes scenarios that may or may not happen (analysis under uncertainty). It's calculated by multiplying the value (impact) of each possible outcome by its probability of occurrence and summing the products.

EMV = Σ (Probability × Impact)

EMV for opportunities is generally expressed as positive values, while EMV for threats is expressed as negative values.

Example: A risk has a 30% chance of causing a $12,000 cost (threat) and a 20% chance of a $5,000 saving (opportunity).

EMV(Threat) = 0.30 × (-$12,000) = -$3,600

EMV(Opportunity) = 0.20 × (+$5,000) = +$1,000

Total EMV for these two = -$3,600 + $1,000 = -$2,600

(Source: Slides: Project Risk Management - Part 2 (Qualitative/Quantitative), Slide 13; CES665 Final Exam 2022, Q8.MCQ1, Q8.MCQ6, Q8.MCQ7, Q8.MCQ14; Mock Exam 10, Q3 SA, Q8 MCQ)

6.4.3 Decision Tree Analysis

A Decision Tree is a diagramming and calculation technique for evaluating the implications of a chain of multiple options in the presence of uncertainty. It helps to choose between different courses of action by calculating the EMV for each branch.

Key components:

  • Decision Nodes (Squares): Represent points where a choice is made.
  • Chance Nodes (Circles): Represent points where uncertain outcomes occur, with associated probabilities.
  • Branches: Represent different choices or outcomes.
  • End Nodes (Triangles or values): Represent the final outcome or payoff of a path.

The tree is "solved" by calculating the EMV at each chance node and then selecting the decision at each decision node that yields the most favorable EMV (highest profit or lowest cost).

Example of a decision tree for "Build or Upgrade" decision, showing EMV calculations. The decision with the higher EMV (e.g., $46M for Upgrade vs. $36M for Build in this example) would be chosen.

(Source: Slides: Project Risk Management - Part 2 (Qualitative/Quantitative), Slides 14-17; CES665 Final Exam 2022, Q8.MCQ11)

6.4.4 Sensitivity Analysis

Sensitivity Analysis helps to determine which individual project risks or other sources of uncertainty have the most potential impact on project objectives. It examines the extent to which the uncertainty of each project element affects the objective being studied when all other uncertain elements are held at their baseline values.

A common way to display the results of sensitivity analysis is a Tornado Diagram. In a tornado diagram:

  • The Y-axis lists the uncertain variables or risks.
  • The X-axis shows the spread or correlation of the uncertainty to the studied output (e.g., project cost or completion date).
  • Bars are ordered by impact, with the largest impact at the top, resembling a tornado.

6.4.5 Modeling and Simulation (Monte Carlo Technique)

Simulation uses a project model that translates the uncertainties specified at a detailed level into their potential impact on project objectives as a whole. Simulations are typically performed using the Monte Carlo technique.

Monte Carlo Technique:

  • A model of the project (e.g., cost estimate or schedule network) is created.
  • For variables with uncertainty (e.g., activity durations, cost items), probability distributions are defined.
  • The model is computed many times (iterated), with input values for each iteration randomly selected from their probability distributions.
  • The results form a probability distribution of possible outcomes for the project (e.g., total cost or completion date).

Outputs can include:

  • An S-curve showing the probability of achieving different cost or schedule targets.
  • Histograms of possible project outcomes.
  • Probability of any activity being on the critical path.
  • Overall risk assessment of the project.

(Source: Slides: Project Risk Management - Part 1, Slides 46-48; Mock Exam 6, MCQ2; Mock Exam 9, MCQ5)

6.5 Outputs of Perform Quantitative Risk Analysis

The main output is updates to Project Documents, primarily the Risk Register and Risk Report. These updates include:

  • Probabilistic analysis of the project: e.g., range of possible completion dates and costs, with associated confidence levels.
  • Probability of achieving cost and time objectives: e.g., "There is an 80% chance of completing the project within $X budget."
  • Prioritized list of quantified individual project risks: Showing which risks pose the greatest threat or offer the greatest opportunity to the project in numerical terms.
  • Trends in quantitative risk analysis results: If analysis is repeated, trends can indicate if overall risk is increasing or decreasing.

(Source: Slides: Project Risk Management - Part 1, Slide 51)
