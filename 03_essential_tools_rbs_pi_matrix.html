Chapter 3: Essential Tools for Risk Identification and Assessment

Effective risk management relies on structured tools and techniques. This chapter introduces two fundamental tools: the Risk Breakdown Structure (RBS) for organizing potential risk sources, and the Probability and Impact Matrix for assessing and prioritizing identified risks.

3.1 Risk Breakdown Structure (RBS)

Definition: Risk Breakdown Structure (RBS) (from Slides & PMBOK® Guide)

The RBS is a hierarchical structure of potential risk sources or categories of risk for a project. (Slide 16)

The PMBOK® Guide describes the RBS as a "hierarchically organized depiction of the identified project risks arranged by risk category and subcategory that identifies the various areas and causes of potential risks." It helps ensure a comprehensive process of risk identification and is useful for categorizing risks in the risk register.

The RBS provides a framework for systematically identifying risks by breaking down the project into various areas where risks might originate. It helps ensure that all significant sources of risk are considered, rather than relying on ad-hoc brainstorming alone.

3.1.1 Characteristics and Purpose of an RBS

  • Hierarchical: Risks are organized in levels, from broad categories to more specific sub-categories or individual risk sources.
  • Tailored: While generic templates exist, an RBS is most effective when tailored to the specific project type, industry, and organizational context.
  • Comprehensive: Aims to cover all significant areas of potential risk.
  • Facilitates Identification: Serves as a prompt list during risk identification workshops.
  • Organizes Risks: Helps in structuring the risk register and grouping similar risks.
  • Improves Communication: Provides a common framework for discussing risks.

Slide 16 mentions key aspects of RBS sources:

  • External/Internal: Risks can originate from outside the project (e.g., market changes, regulatory shifts) or from within (e.g., resource issues, technical challenges).
  • Caused by: Customers, suppliers, or other stakeholders.
  • Source (Impact Area): Often relates to project objectives like cost, time, scope, or quality.

Tip: Using the RBS

The RBS is not just for initial risk identification. It can be used throughout the project to categorize newly identified risks and to ensure that risk monitoring covers all key areas.

3.2 Probability and Impact Matrix

Definition: Probability and Impact Matrix (from Slides & PMBOK® Guide)

It is a grid for mapping the probability of each risk occurrence and its impact on project objectives if that risk occurs. (Slide 17)

The PMBOK® Guide defines it as a "grid for mapping the probability of occurrence of each risk and its impact on project objectives if that risk occurs." This tool is commonly used in Perform Qualitative Risk Analysis to help prioritize risks.

The Probability and Impact (P&I) Matrix is a visual tool that helps in assessing the relative significance of individual risks. By plotting risks on this grid, project teams can quickly identify which risks require the most attention.

3.2.1 Structure of a P&I Matrix

The matrix typically has two axes:

  • Probability (Likelihood): One axis represents the likelihood of a risk occurring (e.g., Very Low, Low, Medium, High, Very High).
  • Impact (Consequence): The other axis represents the potential effect on project objectives if the risk occurs (e.g., Very Low, Low, Medium, High, Very High).

The cells within the matrix represent different levels of risk exposure (or risk score), often color-coded (e.g., green for low risk, yellow/orange for medium risk, red for high risk).

3.2.2 Example P&I Matrix (Conceptual based on Slide 17)

Slide 17 provides a 2x2 conceptual matrix. A more detailed 5x5 matrix is often used in practice:

Conceptual Probability and Impact Matrix
Impact on Objectives
Very Low (0.05) Low (0.1) Moderate (0.2) High (0.4) Very High (0.8)
Probability Very High (0.9) 0.045 0.09 0.18 0.36 0.72

Note: Numerical values (Risk Score = P x I) and color coding are illustrative and should be defined in the Risk Management Plan.

Interpreting the Matrix (from Slide 17)

  • High Impact and Likely to Occur (High Probability): These risks (often in the red zone) require urgent attention and aggressive response strategies.
  • Low Impact and Unlikely to Occur (Low Probability): These risks (often in the green zone) may be accepted or monitored with minimal effort.
  • Other combinations fall into intermediate categories, requiring tailored responses.

The organization must define what constitutes "High," "Medium," and "Low" risk within the matrix, typically documented in the Risk Management Plan.

Chapter 3 Summary: Key Concepts

  • Risk Breakdown Structure (RBS): A hierarchical chart of potential risk sources or categories, used to aid comprehensive risk identification and organization.
  • Purpose of RBS: Ensures systematic risk identification, helps categorize risks, and provides a common framework for discussion.
  • Probability and Impact Matrix (P&I Matrix): A grid used to map the probability and impact of risks, facilitating their prioritization during qualitative risk analysis.
  • Purpose of P&I Matrix: Helps to visually assess risk significance, identify high-priority risks requiring urgent action, and guide risk response planning.
