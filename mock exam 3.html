Section A: Short Answer Questions
Question 1 (5 Marks)
Explain the concept of "Secondary Risk" and provide a relevant project example.

Model Answer:

Secondary Risk: A secondary risk is a new risk that arises as a direct result of implementing a planned risk response to an original risk. When a response strategy is chosen and actioned, it can itself introduce new uncertainties or potential problems.

Example:

A project team identifies a risk that a key software component might be delayed by their in-house development team (original risk). To mitigate this, they decide to transfer the development of this component to an external vendor (risk response). A secondary risk that could arise from this response is "The external vendor may deliver a lower quality component" or "The external vendor might go out of business before completing the work." These new risks are a direct consequence of the decision to transfer the work.

Rationale: Based on Handbook Chapter 7, Slide 52.

Question 2 (5 Marks)
What are the five main steps in a General Safety Inspection process?

Model Answer:

The five main steps in a General Safety Inspection process are:

  1. Prepare: This involves planning the inspection, knowing what to look for, making checklists, reviewing previous reports, and gathering necessary tools.
  2. Inspect: Systematically examining the workplace or area using checklists, observing practices, identifying hazards, and noting positive findings.
  3. Develop Remedial Action: For identified hazards, this involves considering severity, probability, cost of control, and weighing alternatives to determine appropriate corrective actions.
  4. Take Follow-up Actions: Ensuring recommendations are implemented, work orders are issued, progress is monitored, and the adequacy of control is certified.
  5. Prepare Inspection Report: Documenting all findings, hazard classifications, recommended actions, responsibilities, and deadlines.

Rationale: Based on Handbook Chapter 14, Slides 131-133, 137, 138 (and implied report preparation).

Question 3 (5 Marks)
Describe the "Enhance" strategy for positive risks and give an example.

Model Answer:

Enhance Strategy for Positive Risks (Opportunities):

The enhance strategy is used to increase the probability and/or the positive impacts of an opportunity. Unlike the "Exploit" strategy which aims to make the opportunity certain, "Enhance" focuses on improving the chances of the opportunity occurring or increasing its potential benefits if it does materialize. This is achieved by identifying and maximizing the key drivers or causes of these positive-impact risks.

Example:

If a project team identifies an opportunity that a new construction material might become available earlier than expected, potentially speeding up a critical path activity, an "Enhance" strategy could involve:

  • Proactively communicating with the supplier to understand their production schedule and express interest, thereby potentially influencing earlier delivery (increasing probability).
  • Pre-planning logistics for the new material so that if it does arrive early, the team is immediately ready to use it, maximizing the time saved (increasing positive impact).
Another example from the slides is adding more resources to an activity to finish early, thereby increasing the probability of achieving an early completion bonus.

Rationale: Based on Handbook Chapter 8, Slide 65.

Section B: Multiple Choice Questions
4. The output of the Perform Qualitative Risk Analysis process that lists low-priority risks for ongoing monitoring is known as the:
  • A. Risk Register Update
  • B. Watchlist
  • C. Assumption Log
  • D. Risk Report

Correct Answer: B

Rationale: The watchlist is specifically for low-priority risks that are monitored but not actively managed unless their status changes. (Handbook Chapter 5, Section 5.4 and Risk Register Updated slide from other material)

5. In the context of safety management, an unplanned single event, without loss, that has the potential to result in fatality or injury is termed a:
  • A. Hazard
  • B. Accident
  • C. Incident
  • D. Near Miss

Correct Answer: D

Rationale: A Near Miss is an event that did not result in harm but had the potential to do so. (Handbook Chapter 11, Slide 86)

6. Which of the following is a key activity during Risk Reassessment in the Control Risk process?
  • A. Developing the initial Risk Management Plan.
  • B. Assigning risk owners for the first time.
  • C. Closing of risks that are outdated or no longer relevant.
  • D. Performing Monte Carlo simulation for all identified risks.

Correct Answer: C

Rationale: Risk Reassessment involves reviewing current risks, identifying new ones, and closing out risks that are no longer applicable. (Handbook Chapter 9, Slide 69)

7. The "Accident Costs Iceberg" illustrates that:
  • A. Direct costs of accidents are usually the most significant.
  • B. All accident costs are typically covered by insurance.
  • C. Indirect (uninsured) costs of accidents are often much larger than direct costs.
  • D. Property damage is always less costly than injuries.

Correct Answer: C

Rationale: The iceberg model shows that indirect/uninsured costs (hidden below the surface) are often significantly greater than direct/insured costs. (Handbook Chapter 15, Slide 163)

8. The primary purpose of classifying crises (e.g., by origin, category, or matrix) in Crisis Management System development is to:
  • A. Assign blame for the crisis after it occurs.
  • B. Simplify the crisis for media communication.
  • C. Better understand potential crisis exposures and plan appropriate responses.
  • D. Determine the exact financial impact of any crisis.

Correct Answer: C

Rationale: Classifying crises helps in understanding their nature, potential sources, and impacts, which is crucial for effective evaluation and response planning. (Handbook Chapter 16, Slides 179-182)

9. If a project faces a high-impact, high-probability negative risk that cannot be easily mitigated or transferred, and changing the project plan to eliminate it is feasible, the most suitable response strategy would be:
  • A. Accept
  • B. Avoid
  • C. Enhance
  • D. Share

Correct Answer: B

Rationale: Avoidance involves changing the project plan to eliminate the threat entirely, which is suitable for critical risks where other strategies are insufficient and avoidance is feasible. (Handbook Chapter 7, Slide 56)

10. According to the safety management principles, giving priority to solutions protecting the whole workplace (e.g., guardrails) rather than individuals (e.g., harnesses) is an example of:
  • A. Focusing on personal protective equipment first.
  • B. Collective protection over personal protection.
  • C. Adapting work to the individual.
  • D. Combating risks only after they occur.

Correct Answer: B

Rationale: This aligns with the hierarchy of controls, where collective protective measures are preferred over individual ones. (Handbook Chapter 10, Slide 83)
