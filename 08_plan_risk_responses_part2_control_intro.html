Project Risk Management Handbook

Chapter 8: Plan Risk Responses (Part 2 - Acceptance & Positive Risks) and Introduction to Control Risk

This chapter concludes our discussion on planning risk responses by covering the 'Accept' strategy for threats and detailing strategies for positive risks (opportunities). We will then introduce the 'Control Risk' process, which focuses on implementing response plans and monitoring risks throughout the project lifecycle.

8.1 Risk Acceptance Strategy (Slides 62, 63)

Definition: Risk Acceptance

Risk acceptance is a risk response strategy whereby the project team decides to acknowledge the risk and not take any action unless the risk occurs. This strategy is adopted where it is not possible or cost-effective to address a specific risk in any other way. It indicates that the project team has decided not to change the project management plan to deal with a risk, or is unable to identify any other suitable response strategy. (Slide 62)

Acceptance does not mean ignoring the risk; it's a conscious decision made after due consideration. This strategy can be either passive or active:

8.1.1 Passive Acceptance (Slide 63)

  • Action: Requires no action except to document the strategy.
  • Approach: The project team deals with the risks as they occur.
  • Monitoring: Periodically review the threat to ensure that it does not change significantly.
  • Suitability: Often suitable for low-priority risks (low impact and/or low probability).

8.1.2 Active Acceptance (Slide 63)

  • Action: Involves taking proactive steps to prepare for the risk, should it occur.
  • Common Strategy: The most common active acceptance strategy is to establish a contingency reserve, including amounts of time, money, or resources to handle the risks if they materialize.
  • Contingency Plan: Active acceptance is often accompanied by a contingency plan that will be executed if the risk occurs.

Example: Active Acceptance

A project identifies a risk of a minor delay from a non-critical supplier. The team accepts this risk but allocates a small time buffer (contingency reserve) in the schedule and identifies an alternative supplier (contingency plan) just in case the primary supplier fails to deliver.

Exam Focus: Active vs. Passive Acceptance

Understanding the difference between active acceptance (e.g., creating a contingency reserve) and passive acceptance (documenting and moving on) is important for exam questions.

8.2 Strategies for Positive Risks or Opportunities (Slides 64-66)

Just as there are strategies for threats, there are specific strategies to address opportunities (positive risks) to increase their likelihood or impact.

8.2.1 Exploit Strategy (Slide 64)

Definition: Exploit

The exploit strategy may be selected for risks with positive impacts where the organization wishes to ensure that the opportunity is realized. This strategy seeks to eliminate the uncertainty associated with a particular upside risk by ensuring the opportunity definitely happens.

This is an aggressive strategy for high-impact, high-probability opportunities that the project wants to make certain it achieves.

  • Goal: Make the opportunity happen.
  • Action: Take direct measures to ensure the opportunity's conditions are met.
  • Example: Assigning an organization's most talented resources to a project to reduce the time to completion or ensure higher quality, thereby capturing a bonus for early completion.

8.2.2 Enhance Strategy (Slide 65)

Definition: Enhance

The enhance strategy is used to increase the probability and/or the positive impacts of an opportunity. Identifying and maximizing key drivers of these positive-impact risks may increase the probability of their occurrence.

Unlike exploit (which aims for certainty), enhance focuses on increasing the chances or the positive effects if the opportunity occurs.

  • Goal: Increase likelihood or positive impact (or both).
  • Action: Focus on the causes or drivers of the opportunity.
  • Example (from slide): Adding more resources to an activity to finish early (increases the probability of early completion and the positive impact of saved time/cost). Another example could be investing in better technology that might improve productivity more than initially anticipated.

Exploit vs. Enhance

Exploit aims to make the opportunity certain. Enhance aims to increase its probability or positive impact. Exploiting is generally more proactive and resource-intensive than enhancing.

8.2.3 Share Strategy (Slide 66)

Definition: Share

Sharing a positive risk involves allocating some or all of the ownership of the opportunity to a third party who is best able to capture the opportunity for the benefit of the project.

This is similar to transferring a threat, but for an opportunity.

  • Goal: Leverage a third party's capabilities to realize the opportunity.
  • Action: Forming risk-sharing partnerships, teams, special-purpose companies, or joint ventures.
  • Example: A construction company might partner with a specialized technology firm to implement an innovative building technique that could significantly reduce costs and time, sharing the benefits (and potentially the investment).

8.2.4 Accept Strategy (for Opportunities) (Slide 66)

Definition: Accept (for Opportunities)

Accepting an opportunity is being willing to take advantage of the opportunity if it arises, but not actively pursuing it.

Similar to accepting a threat, this involves acknowledging the opportunity but not taking proactive steps to make it happen or enhance it.

  • Goal: Be ready to benefit if the opportunity occurs naturally.
  • Action: No proactive effort beyond documenting and perhaps monitoring.
  • Example: A project team notes that a key supplier might offer an unexpected discount if they have surplus stock. The team accepts this opportunity but doesn't actively negotiate for it, simply remaining prepared to take advantage if the discount is offered.

8.3 Introduction to Control Risk (Slide 68)

Control Risk is the process of implementing risk response plans, tracking identified risks, monitoring residual risks, identifying new risks, and evaluating risk process effectiveness throughout the project. (This definition combines slide information with PMBOK¬Æ concepts).

This is an ongoing process performed throughout the project lifecycle to ensure that risk responses are effective, risks are managed as planned, and new risks are addressed.

8.3.1 Key Activities in Control Risk (Slide 68)

Slide 68 lists several activities involved in controlling risks:

  • Implementing/evaluating the risk response plans.
  • Tracking identified risks.
  • Monitoring residual risks.
  • Identifying new risks.
  • Looking for the occurrence of risk triggers.
  • Collecting/communicating risk status.
  • Determining if assumptions are still valid.
  • Recommending corrective action.
  • Using contingency reserves when needed.
  • Evaluating risk process effectiveness throughout the project.

Continuous Process

Control Risk is not a one-time activity but a continuous effort to manage uncertainty and adapt to changing conditions throughout the project.

The detailed ITTOs (Inputs, Tools & Techniques, Outputs) for Control Risk, shown on Slide 67, will be covered in the next chapter.

Chapter 8 Summary: Key Concepts

  • Risk Acceptance (Threats): Acknowledging a risk without taking action (passive) or setting aside a contingency reserve (active).
  • Strategies for Positive Risks (Opportunities):
    • Exploit: Ensuring the opportunity is realized.
    • Enhance: Increasing the probability and/or positive impacts of an opportunity.
    • Share: Allocating ownership of an opportunity to a third party.
    • Accept: Being willing to take advantage of an opportunity if it arises, but not actively pursuing it.
  • Control Risk: The ongoing process of implementing response plans, tracking risks, monitoring, identifying new risks, and evaluating the effectiveness of the risk management process.
