Question 1 (5 Marks)
Explain the purpose of using a Probability and Impact Matrix in qualitative risk analysis. What key information should be defined in the Risk Management Plan regarding its use?

Model Answer:

Purpose of Probability and Impact (P&I) Matrix:

The P&I Matrix is a tool used in Perform Qualitative Risk Analysis to help prioritize identified individual project risks. Its primary purpose is to assign a risk rating or score to each risk by mapping its assessed probability of occurrence against its potential impact on project objectives if it occurs. This visual tool helps project teams quickly identify which risks require the most urgent attention and guides the type and urgency of risk response actions.

Key Information in Risk Management Plan regarding P&I Matrix use:

  • Definitions of Probability Scales: Clear, qualitative (or sometimes quantitative ranges for qualitative scales) definitions for each level of probability (e.g., Very Low, Low, Medium, High, Very High).
  • Definitions of Impact Scales: Clear, qualitative (or quantitative ranges) definitions for each level of impact across various project objectives (e.g., cost, time, scope, quality). These scales should be tailored to the project context.
  • The P&I Matrix Structure: The actual grid showing how combinations of probability and impact levels translate into specific risk ratings (e.g., Low, Moderate, High risk zones, often color-coded).
  • Risk Score/Rating Thresholds: Definitions of what constitutes high, medium, and low risk based on the matrix, and what actions or further analysis are triggered by each rating.

Rationale: Based on Handbook Chapter 3, Slides 17 & 34; Chapter 5, Slides 34 & 35.

Question 2 (5 Marks)
What are the main Inputs, Tools & Techniques, and Outputs (ITTOs) for the "Plan Risk Responses" process?

Model Answer:

Inputs to Plan Risk Responses:

  • Project Management Plan (especially Risk Management Plan, Resource Management Plan, Scope Baseline, Cost Baseline)
  • Project Documents (e.g., Risk Register, Risk Report, Lessons Learned Register, Stakeholder Register)
  • Enterprise Environmental Factors (EEFs)
  • Organizational Process Assets (OPAs)

Tools & Techniques for Plan Risk Responses:

  • Expert Judgment
  • Data Gathering (e.g., Interviews)
  • Interpersonal and Team Skills (e.g., Facilitation)
  • Strategies for Threats (Avoid, Transfer, Mitigate, Accept)
  • Strategies for Opportunities (Exploit, Share, Enhance, Accept)
  • Contingent Response Strategies (developing contingency plans)
  • Data Analysis (e.g., Alternatives Analysis, Cost-Benefit Analysis)
  • Decision Making (e.g., Multicriteria decision analysis)

Outputs of Plan Risk Responses:

  • Change Requests
  • Project Management Plan Updates (e.g., updates to baselines, subsidiary plans)
  • Project Documents Updates (especially Risk Register with response plans, owners, residual risks, secondary risks; Risk Report)

Rationale: Based on Handbook Chapter 7, Slide 54 and PMBOK principles.

Question 3 (5 Marks)
What is the "Accident Pyramid" (or Safety Triangle) and what key lessons can be learned from it for safety management?

Model Answer:

Accident Pyramid (Safety Triangle):

The Accident Pyramid is a model that illustrates the typical statistical relationship between the number of workplace incidents of varying severity. It generally shows that for every major injury (or fatality) at the top of the pyramid, there are a larger number of minor injuries, an even larger number of property damage incidents, and a very large base of near misses or incidents with no loss/injury.

A common representation (as seen in the slides) might be: 1 Major Accident, 10 Minor Accidents, 30 Property Damage Incidents, and 600 Near Misses/Incidents with No Loss.

Key Lessons for Safety Management:

  1. Near Misses are Warning Signs: The large base of near misses indicates numerous opportunities for learning and prevention. These "free lessons" highlight underlying hazards and unsafe conditions that, if unaddressed, could eventually lead to more severe accidents.
  2. Common Root Causes: The underlying causes and contributing factors for near misses are often the same as those for more serious accidents. Addressing the root causes of near misses can prevent major incidents.
  3. Importance of Reporting All Incidents: To leverage the learning potential of the pyramid, a strong reporting culture for all events, especially near misses, is crucial.
  4. Focus on Proactive Prevention: By focusing on preventing the more frequent, less severe incidents at the base of the pyramid, organizations can significantly reduce the likelihood of the rare but catastrophic events at the top.
  5. High Potential (HI-PO) Incidents: Even if an event didn't result in a major loss, if it had the potential to, it should be investigated thoroughly as a high-potential incident.

Rationale: Based on Handbook Chapter 15, Slides 140, 141, 162.

5. During planning processes, you used Monte Carlo simulation to quantitatively assess cost and schedule risks of your project. During risk control, you repeat the technique, and it leads to different results. What should NOT be the reason for that?
  • A. Some assumptions during planning have meanwhile become fact-based knowledge, so that the risks related to them have vanished or have become certain problems.
  • B. New risks have been identified. This influence the input data used for Monte Carlo simulation in a way which was not predictable at the time when the simulation was run.
  • C. Some constraints have been identified originally, but their influence on the project was unclear when the simulation was run for the first time. By now, the team understands these constraints much better and has been able to adjust the simulation.
  • D. The Monte Carlo software was updated to a newer version with a different random number generator.

Correct Answer: D (Assuming the question implies reasons related to project changes, not tool changes)

Rationale: Options A, B, and C describe valid project-related changes (resolved assumptions, new risks, better understood constraints) that would alter the inputs to a Monte Carlo simulation and thus lead to different results. Option D, while technically could lead to different results, refers to a change in the analysis tool itself rather than a change in the project's risk landscape or data, which is usually the focus when comparing simulation runs over time in risk control. The original exam option D was "Some dummy activities... get bigger over time," which was also a less direct reason. This rephrased D is more clearly about the tool. If sticking to the original options, the original D was the best 'should not be the reason'.

6. When is passive risk acceptance an appropriate approach?
  • A. When the likeliness of risk occurrence is very low (and/or impact is very low).
  • B. When the risk occurrence is hard to identify.
  • C. When it is best to deal with a risk as it occurs, regardless of its priority.
  • D. When the risk cannot be assessed or analyzed.

Correct Answer: A

Rationale: Passive acceptance is typically appropriate for low-priority risks, meaning those with a very low probability of occurrence and/or very low impact. If a risk is hard to identify (B) or cannot be assessed (D), further investigation is needed, not necessarily passive acceptance. Dealing with all risks as they occur (C) is reactive and not strategic. (Handbook Chapter 8, Slide 63)

7. Which statement is most accurate regarding how organizations perceive risk?
  • A. Organizations are not likely to perceive risk as a threat to project success.
  • B. Risk management is primarily a passive activity in project management.
  • C. Risk has its origins in the uncertainty inherent in all projects.
  • D. Risk only has negative impacts on projects.

Correct Answer: C

Rationale: A is false; organizations do perceive threats. B is false; risk management is proactive. D is false; risks can be opportunities. C is true; uncertainty is the fundamental basis of risk. (Handbook Chapter 1, Slide 5 & 6)

8. Which of these is a valid response strategy specifically for negative risks (threats) and NOT for positive risks (opportunities)?
  • A. Exploit
  • B. Mitigate
  • C. Enhance
  • D. Share

Correct Answer: B

Rationale: Mitigate is a strategy to reduce the probability and/or impact of negative risks. Exploit and Enhance are for opportunities. Share can be used for opportunities (sharing benefits) and sometimes for threats (as in transferring part of a negative risk). (Handbook Chapter 7, Slide 57 & Chapter 8, Slides 64-66)
