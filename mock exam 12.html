Question 1 (5 Marks)
What are the main aims of a Crisis Management System (CMS)?

Model Answer:

The main aims of a Crisis Management System (CMS) are:

  • Ensure proper Management of Crises: To provide a structured and effective approach to handling crisis situations when they occur.
  • Enable Business Continuity: To help the organization continue its essential operations or recover them as quickly as possible during and after a crisis, minimizing disruption.
  • Allow the company to respond to unexpected events or situations: To equip the organization to deal effectively with unforeseen events that have potentially negative effects.
  • Protect Resources and People: To minimize harm to personnel, damage to assets, and negative impacts on financial resources.
  • Preserve Reputation and Success: To mitigate damage to the organization's reputation and ensure the continued success and operation of its facilities or projects.

Rationale: Based on Handbook Chapter 16, Slide 176.

Question 2 (5 Marks)
List and briefly describe the five stages of the Loss Causation Model.

Model Answer:

The five stages of the Loss Causation Model are:

  1. Lack of Control: Deficiencies in the management system, such as inadequate safety programs, standards, or compliance with standards. These are the ultimate root causes.
  2. Basic Causes (Root Causes): The underlying reasons why immediate causes exist. These are typically categorized into:
    • Personal Factors: e.g., lack of knowledge/skill, improper motivation, physical/mental problems.
    • System/Job Factors: e.g., inadequate leadership, poor design, inadequate maintenance, insufficient tools/equipment.
  3. Immediate Causes (Direct Causes/Symptoms): The unsafe acts and/or unsafe conditions that directly preceded and led to the incident. These are the most visible causes.
    • Substandard Acts (Unsafe Acts): e.g., operating without authority, failure to use PPE.
    • Substandard Conditions (Unsafe Conditions): e.g., inadequate guards, defective equipment.
  4. Incident (The Accident Event): The actual event where contact is made with a source of energy or substance above the threshold limit of the body or structure (e.g., struck by, fall, caught in).
  5. Loss: The undesired outcome of the incident, such as injury, illness, death, property damage, environmental damage, or production delays.

Rationale: Based on Handbook Chapter 15, Slides 158, 160, 161, 164-167.

Question 3 (5 Marks)
You are managing a construction project and have identified three key risk factors with associated probabilities and impacts (cost/schedule). Briefly describe how you would calculate the Expected Monetary Value (EMV) for one of these risk factors and how this EMV helps in decision-making. (Assume a risk factor with P=0.3 and Impact Cost=$50,000).

Model Answer:

Calculating Expected Monetary Value (EMV):

For a given risk factor, the Expected Monetary Value (EMV) is calculated by multiplying the probability (P) of the risk occurring by its financial impact (I) if it does occur.

For the example risk factor (P=0.3, Impact Cost=$50,000):

EMV = Probability × Impact

EMV = 0.3 × (-$50,000) (Impact is negative as it's a cost)

EMV = -$15,000

How EMV Helps in Decision-Making:

  • Risk Prioritization: EMV provides a quantitative measure to rank risks. Risks with higher negative EMVs (for threats) or higher positive EMVs (for opportunities) are generally given higher priority.
  • Response Strategy Selection: The EMV can help decide if the cost of a risk response is justified. For example, if a mitigation action costs $5,000 but reduces the EMV of a threat from -$15,000 to -$2,000, it might be considered worthwhile.
  • Contingency Reserve Estimation: The sum of EMVs for various identified risks can be one input into determining the overall contingency reserve needed for the project.
  • Decision Tree Analysis: EMV is a fundamental component in decision tree analysis, helping to choose the most financially favorable path among different alternatives involving uncertainty.
  • Communicating Risk Severity: It provides a common, quantifiable language for discussing the potential financial implications of risks with stakeholders.

Rationale: Based on Handbook Chapter 6 (EMV concepts) and general risk management principles.

4. Your risk analysis involving your project scope reflects your project has a 70% chance of completing the scope by a specified date AND an 80% chance of receiving approval for the scope by a certain date. Assuming these are independent events, what is the probability of both events occurring?
  • A. 0.75
  • B. 0.80
  • C. 0.50
  • D. 0.56

Correct Answer: D

Rationale: For independent events, the probability of both occurring is the product of their individual probabilities. P(A and B) = P(A) * P(B). So, 0.70 * 0.80 = 0.56. (General probability theory)

  • 1. Exploit
  • 2. Transfer
  • 3. Mitigate
  • 4. Avoid

Correct Answer: 1

She knows that a competitor is about to break ground in the adjacent lot and contacts him to discuss the possibility of having both projects save costs by building a sewage system for the two projects. This is an example of which strategy for an opportunity?
  • 1. Mitigate
  • 2. Share
  • 3. Accept
  • 4. Enhance

Correct Answer: 2 (or potentially Exploit if that was an option)

This aligns best with the "Share" strategy for positive risks. "Exploit" could also be argued if the PM is actively trying to make this happen. (Handbook Chapter 8, Slide 66)

8. You are managing a construction project. There is a 30% chance that the weather will cause a three-day delay, costing $12,000. There is also a 20% chance that the price of your building materials will drop, which will save $5,000. What is the total EMV for both of these?
  • A. -$3,600
  • B. $1,000
  • C. -$2,600
  • D. $4,600

Correct Answer: C

Rationale: EMV (Delay) = 0.30 * (-$12,000) = -$3,600. EMV (Price Drop) = 0.20 * (+$5,000) = +$1,000. Total EMV = -$3,600 + $1,000 = -$2,600. (Handbook Chapter 6)

  • 3. Work performance information

10. Which of the following statements is true about risks?
  • A. Risks always have negative impact and not positive.
  • B. Risk impact should be considered, but probability of occurrence is not important.
  • C. The risk register documents all the identified risks in detail.
  • D. Risk Response Plan is another name for Risk Management Plan.

Correct Answer: C

Rationale: A is false (risks can be positive opportunities). B is false (both probability and impact are crucial). D is false (Risk Management Plan outlines *how* to do risk management, while Risk Response Plan details specific actions for identified risks). C is true; the risk register is the comprehensive log of identified risks and their details. (Handbook Chapter 1, Slide 6; Chapter 4, Slide 29)
