Project Risk Management Handbook

Chapter 10: Fundamentals of Health and Safety in Projects

Beyond managing risks to project objectives like time, cost, and scope, ensuring the Health and Safety (H&S) of all personnel and stakeholders is a paramount responsibility in project management, especially in industries like construction. This chapter introduces fundamental H&S concepts, planning, and considerations.

10.1 Defining Safety (Slide 75)

What is Safety?

Based on Slide 75, safety can be understood as:

  • The absence of danger.
  • A state of protection.
  • A risk-free condition.

It's important to also consider health besides safety, as occupational health hazards can have long-term impacts even if they don't result in immediate injury.

In a project context, safety involves proactive measures to prevent accidents, injuries, and occupational illnesses. It's about creating an environment where work can be performed without undue risk to individuals.

10.2 Hazards in Construction Projects (Reference to Slide 76)

Construction sites are inherently hazardous environments. Slide 76 ("Top 16 Hazards in Construction sites") provides a visual overview of common dangers. While the specific hazards vary by project, common categories include:

  • Falls from height (e.g., from scaffolding, roofs).
  • Struck-by incidents (e.g., by moving equipment, falling objects).
  • Electrocution.
  • Caught-in or -between hazards (e.g., in machinery, collapsing trenches).
  • Unsafe use of equipment and machinery.
  • Hazardous materials exposure.
  • Fire and explosions.
  • Structural collapses.

Slide 76 illustrates various common construction site hazards. Effective H&S planning requires identifying and mitigating such site-specific hazards.

Identifying these hazards is a critical first step in safety management, forming the basis for risk assessments and control measures.

10.3 Key Health & Safety Terminology (Slide 77)

Understanding basic H&S terminology is crucial for clear communication and effective safety management.

Key Terms

  • Accident: An unplanned event that results in injury to people, damage to property, or harm to the environment.
  • Injury: Physical harm or damage to a person's body resulting from an accident or exposure to a hazard.
  • Hazard: A potential source of harm or adverse health effect on a person or persons. It is a condition or practice with the potential to cause loss.
  • Threat (in H&S context): Often used interchangeably with hazard, referring to something that can cause harm or loss.

(Note: Some of these terms, like "Hazard" and "Risk," will be explored in greater detail in the subsequent "Safety Management" section based on Prof. Dr. ENG. Khaled Ahmed Hamdy's slides.)

    10.5 The Health and Safety Plan (Slides 79, 80)

    A formal Health and Safety (H&S) Plan is essential for managing safety on a project.

    10.5.1 Purpose of the H&S Plan (Slide 79)

    • To assist and contribute to the establishment of a safe, healthy, and environmentally sound working environment.
    • To benefit all workers, management personnel on site, neighbors, and third parties.
    • To minimize the environmental impact of the work.

    10.5.2 Content of the H&S Plan (Slide 79)

    The plan should include:

    • Details of the project.
    • Safety, health, and environmental requirements.
    • Anticipated site hazards and conditions.
    • Means of controlling these hazards and conditions.

    10.5.3 Responsibility and Development (Slide 80)

    • Contractor Responsibility: "It must be the responsibility of every contractor to be aware of the Safety Plan and its content, and to make known to their workers all specific provisions which they are required to comply with."
    • Availability at Bid Stage: "When the Safety Plan is prepared and available at the bid stage, it allows contractors foresight of conditions which may alter their bids." This promotes transparency and allows contractors to price safety measures appropriately.
    • Prerequisite - Risk Assessment: "The Safety Plan cannot be developed until a project risk assessment has been made, in order to list all the issues and obtain an overview of significant hazards and risks, and to specify solutions to them if possible."

    10.6 Pre-tender Health and Safety Plan (Slide 81)

    Definition: Pre-tender H&S Plan

    A summary of health and safety matters that should be addressed at the pre-tender stage (before bids are submitted). It provides potential bidders with crucial H&S information relevant to the project.

    Key components to be covered in a Pre-tender H&S Plan include:

    • General description and location of the work.
    • Time and program (schedule).
    • Existing off-site conditions (e.g., traffic, public access).
    • Existing on-site conditions (e.g., ground conditions, existing structures, utilities).
    • Hazards from existing structures (especially for demolition and refurbishment).
    • Existing records (e.g., asbestos surveys, utility drawings).
    • The design (highlighting any inherent H&S considerations).
    • Site layout and management proposals.
    • Site rules and procedures.

    10.7 Developing the Construction Phase Health and Safety Plan (Slides 82, 83)

    This plan is typically developed by the main contractor and focuses on practical implementation of safety measures during construction. Key principles and focus areas include:

    10.7.1 Core Principles (Slide 82)

    • Avoid risks altogether where possible (elimination).
    • Evaluate risks that cannot be avoided (risk assessment).
    • Combat risks at source (e.g., removing a problem rather than just posting a warning sign). This aligns with the hierarchy of controls.
    • Adapt work to the individual, and give them control over speed of work where possible (ergonomics and worker well-being).
    • Make use of technological progress to improve safety.

    10.7.2 Further Principles (Slide 83)

    • Give priority to solutions protecting the whole workplace rather than individuals (e.g., guardrails are preferred over individual safety harnesses, representing collective protection over personal protection).
    • Make sure everyone knows what they need to do to remain safe and healthy (communication, training, and information).
    • Achieve a positive organizational safety culture where risk control is accepted as a normal part of life rather than an add-on.

    Hierarchy of Controls

    Many of these principles align with the widely recognized "Hierarchy of Controls" in safety management: Elimination, Substitution, Engineering Controls, Administrative Controls, and Personal Protective Equipment (PPE). The plan should prioritize controls higher up this hierarchy.

    Chapter 10 Summary: Key Concepts

    • Safety: The absence of danger, a state of protection, and a risk-free condition, encompassing both immediate safety and long-term health.
    • Hazards: Potential sources of harm common in construction (e.g., falls, struck-by).
    • Unsafe Acts: Worker behaviors that deviate from safe practices and can lead to accidents.
    • Health and Safety Plan: A formal document detailing project H&S requirements, anticipated hazards, and control measures. It's crucial from the bid stage through construction.
    • Pre-tender H&S Plan: Provides H&S information to bidders.
    • Construction Phase H&S Plan: Developed by the main contractor, focusing on avoiding/evaluating risks, combating them at source, and fostering a positive safety culture.
