Chapter 4: Identify Risks

Risk identification is the process of determining which risks might affect the project and documenting their characteristics. It's a crucial step because a risk cannot be managed unless it is first identified.

(Source: Slides: Project Risk Management - Part 1, Slide 19)

Key Principles of Risk Identification

  • Iterative Process: Risk identification is not a one-time event. It should be performed throughout the project lifecycle as new risks may emerge or existing ones may change.
  • Impossibility of Early Full Identification: It's impossible to identify all risks at the beginning of a project. Project risk exposure changes due to internal decisions and external factors.
  • Early Response Consideration: When a risk is first identified, potential responses may also be identified for immediate action if appropriate.

(Source: Slides: Project Risk Management - Part 1, Slide 19)

4.1 Success Factors for Risk Identification

The success of the risk identification process depends on several factors:

  • ✓Early identification
  • ✓Iterative identification
  • ✓Emergent identification (being open to new risks)
  • ✓Comprehensive identification (striving to identify all relevant risks)
  • ✓Explicit identification of opportunities (not just threats)
  • ✓Multiple perspectives (involving diverse stakeholders)
  • ✓Risks linked to project objectives
  • ✓Complete risk statement (clear articulation of cause, risk, effect)
  • ✓Ownership and level of detail
  • ✓Objectivity (minimizing bias)

(Source: Slides: Project Risk Management - Part 1, Slide 20; CES665 Final Exam 2022, Q2)

4.3 Stakeholder Identification and Classification

Identifying and engaging stakeholders is crucial as they are a primary source for risk identification and can be significantly impacted by risks. Stakeholders can be classified to prioritize engagement efforts, often using a Power/Interest grid.

Power/Interest Grid: High Power/High Interest (Manage Closely), High Power/Low Interest (Keep Satisfied), Low Power/High Interest (Keep Informed), Low Power/Low Interest (Monitor).

(Source: Slides: Project Risk Management - Part 1, Slides 22, 24; Mock Exam 8, MCQ5)

4.4 Key Risk Identification Techniques

4.4.1 Brainstorming

Objective: To obtain a comprehensive list of project risks.

Process: Typically done by a project team, often with outside experts (multidisciplinary). Ideas about risks are generated under the leadership of a facilitator. A Risk Breakdown Structure (RBS) can be used as a guideline. The focus is on generating many ideas without immediate criticism.

(Source: Slides: Project Risk Management - Part 1, Slide 25; Mock Exam 4, MCQ10)

4.4.2 Delphi Technique

Process: Project risk experts participate anonymously. A facilitator uses a questionnaire to solicit ideas about important project risks. Responses are summarized and re-circulated to the experts for further comment. Consensus is typically reached in a few rounds. This technique helps avoid bias from dominant personalities.

(Source: Slides: Project Risk Management - Part 1, Slide 26; Mock Exam 6, MCQ1; Mock Exam 7, MCQ4)

4.4.3 SWOT Analysis

Process: Examines the project from four perspectives:

  • Strengths (Internal, Positive): What does the project do well?
  • Weaknesses (Internal, Negative): Where does the project need improvement?
  • Opportunities (External, Positive): What external factors can the project leverage?
  • Threats (External, Negative): What external factors could harm the project?

This helps identify both positive (opportunities) and negative (threats) risks arising from internal and external factors.

(Source: Slides: Project Risk Management - Part 1, Slide 27; Mock Exam 9, Q1 SA)

4.4.4 Other Information Gathering & Analysis Techniques

  • Interviewing: Directly questioning project participants, stakeholders, and Subject Matter Experts (SMEs) to identify risks based on their experience and insights.
  • Root Cause Analysis: A technique to identify the underlying causes of risks, rather than just the symptoms. This helps in developing more effective responses.
  • Checklist Analysis: Using checklists developed from historical information, lessons learned from similar projects, or the lowest level of an RBS. This ensures common risks are not overlooked but should not limit thinking to only listed items.
  • Assumptions Analysis: Exploring the validity of project assumptions and identifying risks that may arise from inaccuracy, inconsistency, or incompleteness of these assumptions.

(Source: Slides: Project Risk Management - Part 1, Slide 28; Mock Exam 2, Q2 SA)

4.5 Output: Risk Register (Initial)

The primary output of the Identify Risks process is the initial Risk Register. This document is a tool for documenting identified risks and the actions to manage each risk. Initially, it typically contains:

  • List of Identified Risks: Each risk with a unique identifier.
  • Risk Description: Clear statement of the risk (cause, risk event, effect).
  • Risk Category: (e.g., from RBS).
  • Potential Risk Owners: Initial assignment of responsibility.
  • Potential Risk Responses: Preliminary ideas for addressing the risk.
  • Root causes (if identified).
  • Impact (preliminary assessment).
  • Probability (preliminary assessment).

The Risk Register is a living document and will be updated throughout subsequent risk management processes.

(Source: Slides: Project Risk Management - Part 1, Slide 29; Mock Exam 2, Q2 SA; Mock Exam 8, MCQ6)

Chapter 5: Perform Qualitative Risk Analysis

Perform Qualitative Risk Analysis is the process of prioritizing identified individual project risks for further analysis or action by assessing their probability of occurrence and impact, as well as other characteristics.

Purpose of Qualitative Analysis

It's a subjective analysis aimed at:

  • Determining the probability and impact of all identified risks in a subjective manner.
  • Deciding which risks warrant further, more detailed analysis (e.g., quantitative analysis) or immediate response planning.
  • Documenting non-critical risks for a watchlist.
  • Determining the overall risk ranking for the project.

(Source: Slides: Project Risk Management - Part 1, Slide 30; Mock Exam 2, MCQ7)

5.1 Critical Success Factors

For effective qualitative risk analysis, certain factors are critical:

  • Use an agreed-upon approach (for assessing probability, impact, urgency, manageability, external impact).
  • Use agreed-upon definitions of risk terms.
  • Collect high-quality information about risks.
  • Perform iterative qualitative risk analysis throughout the project.

Hierarchy of success factors for qualitative analysis.

(Source: Slides: Project Risk Management - Part 1, Slide 31)

5.3 Key Tools & Techniques

5.3.1 Risk Probability and Impact Assessment

This involves assessing the likelihood (probability) that each specific risk will occur and the potential effect (impact) on project objectives if it does occur. These assessments can be done through interviews, meetings, or using expert judgment. Qualitative terms (e.g., Very High, High, Medium, Low, Very Low) are often used.

(Source: Slides: Project Risk Management - Part 1, Slide 33)

5.3.2 Probability and Impact (P&I) Matrix

A P&I Matrix is constructed to assign risk ratings (e.g., low, moderate, high) by combining probability and impact scales. The organization defines these scales and how combinations result in specific risk ratings (often color-coded: red for high, yellow for moderate, green for low). This helps prioritize risks for response.

Example P&I Matrix with numerical scores for threats. Similar matrices can be developed for opportunities.

Risks with high probability and high impact require further analysis (possibly quantitative) and aggressive management. Lower-priority risks may be placed on a watchlist.

(Source: Slides: Project Risk Management - Part 1, Slides 17, 34, 35; Mock Exam 6, Q1 SA)

5.3.3 Risk Data Quality Assessment

Accurate data is necessary for reliable qualitative risk analysis. Before using any data, the Project Manager should check its accuracy and integration. Assessment involves examining:

  • Extent of understanding of a risk.
  • Data available about the risk.
  • Reliability of data.

If data quality is poor, more effort may be needed to gather better data, or the uncertainty associated with the assessment should be acknowledged.

(Source: Slides: Project Risk Management - Part 2 (Qualitative/Quantitative), Slide 2)

5.3.4 Risk Categorization

Grouping risks with common characteristics can lead to developing more effective risk responses. Risks can be categorized by:

  • Source of risk: Using a Risk Breakdown Structure (RBS) (e.g., Technical, Financial).
  • Area of project affected: Using a Work Breakdown Structure (WBS).
  • Project phase: (e.g., Planning, Implementation).
  • Location: (e.g., specific geographical areas like Egypt, Gulf).

(Source: Slides: Project Risk Management - Part 2 (Qualitative/Quantitative), Slide 3)

5.3.5 Risk Urgency Assessment

This assessment applies to risks that may move more quickly than others or require a near-term response. Urgent risks may move independently to risk response planning, while others might proceed through quantitative analysis if planned. Urgency helps prioritize risks that need immediate attention, even if their P&I score isn't the absolute highest.

Conceptual scale for risk urgency from Low (Insignificant) to High (Critical).

(Source: Slides: Project Risk Management - Part 2 (Qualitative/Quantitative), Slide 4)

5.4 Outputs of Perform Qualitative Risk Analysis

The primary output is updates to project documents, most notably the Risk Register. These updates include:

  • Relative ranking or priority list of project risks: This is the main outcome, guiding focus.
  • Risks grouped by categories: Helps identify concentrations of risk.
  • List of risks requiring response in the near-term: Based on urgency and priority.
  • Watchlist of low-priority risks: These are monitored but not actively managed unless their status changes.
  • List of risks for additional analysis and response (e.g., those moving to quantitative analysis).
  • Basis of assessment (rationale for P&I ratings).

(Source: Slides: Project Risk Management - Part 2 (Qualitative/Quantitative), Slide 5; Mock Exam 3, Q1 MCQ; Mock Exam 3, Q2 SA)

Possible Outcomes of Qualitative Risk Analysis

Beyond updating the risk register, qualitative analysis can lead to broader project decisions:

  • The project can be compared to the overall risks of other projects.
  • The project could be selected, continued, or terminated based on its risk profile.
  • Resources could be moved between projects.
  • A full benefit/cost analysis of the project may be able to be completed.
  • Trends in project risk can be identified if risk qualification is repeated.

(Source: Slides: Project Risk Management - Part 2 (Qualitative/Quantitative), Slide 6)
