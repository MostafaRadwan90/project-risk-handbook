Chapter 14: Safety Inspections

Safety inspections are a proactive and fundamental component of any effective safety management system. They involve critically examining the workplace for hazards, non-compliance with safety standards, and areas for improvement. This chapter details the purpose, types, and processes involved in conducting thorough safety inspections.

14.1 Purpose of Safety Inspections (Slide 120)

Safety inspections help identify various issues before they lead to incidents. According to Slide 120, they help identify:

  • Potential Problems: Unforeseen hazards or developing issues.
  • Equipment Deficiencies: Faulty, damaged, or improperly maintained tools and machinery.
  • Improper Employee Actions: Unsafe behaviors or deviations from safe work procedures.
  • Effects of Change: How modifications in processes, equipment, or environment impact safety.
  • Inadequate Remedial Actions: Failures or shortcomings in previously implemented corrective actions.
  • Positive Performance: Good safety practices that can be reinforced and shared. (This is an important, often overlooked aspect).

14.2 Types of Inspections (Slide 121)

Inspections can be categorized as informal or planned, each serving different purposes.

Informal Inspections

  • Supervisor's Walkthrough: Regular, often unscheduled, checks by supervisors of their work areas. Helps maintain ongoing safety awareness.
  • Employee Condition Report: Workers reporting observed hazards or unsafe conditions. Encouraging such reporting is vital.

Planned Inspections

These are systematic and scheduled inspections, often using checklists and documented procedures. Types include:

  • Critical Parts Inspections: Focus on components whose failure could lead to major problems (covered in detail below).
  • Pre-use Inspections: Checks performed by operators before using equipment.
  • Preventive Maintenance Inspections: Part of a scheduled maintenance program to ensure equipment reliability and safety.
  • Housekeeping Inspections: Assessing orderliness, cleanliness, and general site conditions.
  • General Safety and Health Inspections: Comprehensive reviews of overall safety compliance in an area or across a site.

14.3 Critical Parts/Items Inspections (Slides 122-126, 128)

Definition: Critical Parts/Items (Slide 122)

The components of machinery, equipment, materials, structures, or areas more likely than other components to result in a major problem or loss when worn, damaged, abused, misused, or improperly applied.

14.3.1 Managing Critical Parts Inspection (Slide 123)

A systematic approach includes:

  1. Inventory the Critical Parts: Identify and list all critical components.
  2. Set Inspection Schedules: Determine the frequency and timing of inspections based on factors like usage, environment, and manufacturer recommendations.
  3. Ensure Performance to Standards: Verify that inspections are conducted thoroughly and findings are addressed according to established standards.

14.3.2 Critical Parts Inventory (Slide 124)

Developing this inventory involves:

  • Categorize everything (e.g., by equipment type, area).
  • Delineate areas of responsibility for different critical parts.
  • List all units in each category.
  • Systematically identify the critical parts within those units.
  • List all critical parts in a record system (e.g., a critical parts register).

14.3.3 Critical Parts Inspection Scheduling (Slide 125)

  • Compile record cards or digital records for each critical part.
  • Notify appropriate inspector(s).
  • Use normal work orders or a dedicated system for scheduling and tracking.
  • Communicate the importance of the inspection to all involved.

14.3.4 Critical Parts Inspection Follow-up (Slide 126)

  • Spot-check record cards for completeness and accuracy.
  • Sample follow-up inspections to verify the quality of initial inspections.
  • Review incidents for any involvement of critical parts (to refine the critical parts list or inspection frequency).
  • Verify appropriate scheduling is being maintained.

14.3.5 Critical Parts vs. Preventive Maintenance Inspections (Slide 128)

Critical Parts Inspection

Checks parts in which failure or malfunction could cause a significant loss (to People, Property, Process).

Preventive Maintenance Inspections

Checks parts which wear out, need lubrication or service, or require adjustment to prevent failure.

Slide 128 notes that: Some parts need Critical Parts Inspection, some need Preventive Maintenance Inspections, and some need both.

14.4 Pre-Use Inspections (Slide 129)

Definition: Pre-Use Inspections

Operator or machine checks of systems which are vital to proper, safe operation and which could be damaged or become substandard by regular use.

Implementing pre-use inspections involves:

  • Designate equipment requiring pre-use checks.
  • Provide forms or checklists for operators.
  • Train operators on how to perform the checks correctly.
  • Audit compliance to ensure checks are being done.

Example: Pre-Use Inspection

A forklift operator performing daily checks on brakes, horn, lights, and hydraulic systems before starting work.

14.5 Order and Housekeeping (Slide 130)

What is "Order"?

A place is in order when "there are no unnecessary things around" and when "all necessary things are in their proper places". (Slide 130)

The slide emphasizes: "No" in this sentence means None! Not Any! ‚Äì Not Even One!

Essentially: Everything in place & Nothing out of place.

Good housekeeping is fundamental to safety. Poor housekeeping can lead to slips, trips, falls, fire hazards, and obstructed access/egress. Housekeeping inspections (mentioned on Slide 121) focus on maintaining an orderly work environment.

14.6 Steps of a General Inspection (Slides 131-133, 137, 138)

A systematic approach to general safety inspections involves several key steps:

  1. Prepare (Slide 132)
    • Start with a positive attitude.
    • Plan the inspection (scope, area, focus).
    • Know what to look for (relevant standards, procedures, common hazards).
    • Make checklists (can be based on regulations, past incidents, specific site needs).
    • Review previous inspection reports.
    • Get tools and materials needed (e.g., checklist, camera, measuring devices).
  2. Inspect (Slide 133)
    • Use a floor plan and checklist.
    • Mention the positive findings as well (reinforces good practices).
    • Look for off-the-floor and out-of-the-way items (hidden hazards).
    • Take necessary temporary actions for immediate dangers (e.g., cordon off an area).
    • Describe and locate each item/hazard clearly.
    • Classify the hazards (see Section 14.7).
    • Report unnecessary items (related to order/housekeeping).
    • Determine the basic causes of identified hazards/non-compliances.
  3. Develop Remedial Action (Slide 137)
    • Consider potential severity of loss.
    • Evaluate probability of occurrence.
    • Estimate Cost of Control (cost to fix the hazard).
    • Assess potential degree of control achievable.
    • Weigh control alternatives (e.g., using the hierarchy of controls).
    • Justify recommended control/remedial action.
  4. Take Follow-up Actions (Slide 138)
    • Ensure commendations for positive findings are given.
    • Write work orders for corrective actions.
    • Monitor resources allocated for corrections.
    • Verify timely actions are taken.
    • Monitor progress of corrective actions.
    • Certify adequacy of control once actions are completed.
    • Conduct a final review.
  5. Prepare Inspection Report (Implied by "Review previous reports" and general good practice)
    • Document findings, hazard classifications, recommended actions, responsibilities, and deadlines.

14.7 Hazard Classification (Slides 134-136)

Classifying hazards by their potential severity helps prioritize corrective actions.

Hazard Classification (based on Slides 134-136)
Class Severity Level Description
Class A Hazard Major A condition or practice likely to cause permanent disability, loss of life or body part and/or extensive loss of structure, equipment or material. Can be called "significant".
Class B Hazard Serious A condition or practice likely to cause serious injury or illness, resulting in temporary disability or property damage that is disruptive but not extensive. May be "significant".
Class C Hazard Minor A condition or practice likely to cause minor, non-disabling injury or illness or non-disruptive property damage. Not "significant".

Significance

The term "significant" is used for Class A and potentially Class B hazards, indicating they require more urgent attention and robust control measures.

Chapter 14 Summary: Key Concepts

  • Safety Inspections: Proactive examinations to identify potential problems, equipment deficiencies, unsafe acts, and inadequate remedial actions, and also to note positive performance.
  • Types of Inspections: Informal (walkthroughs, employee reports) and Planned (Critical Parts, Pre-use, Preventive Maintenance, Housekeeping, General Safety).
  • Critical Parts Inspections: Focus on components whose failure could cause significant loss; involve inventory, scheduling, and follow-up.
  • Pre-Use Inspections: Operator checks before using equipment.
  • General Inspection Steps: Prepare, Inspect, Develop Remedial Action, Take Follow-up Actions, Prepare Report.
  • Hazard Classification: Categorizing hazards by severity (Class A - Major, Class B - Serious, Class C - Minor) to prioritize actions.
