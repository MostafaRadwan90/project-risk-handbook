Core Concept: Plan Risk Responses

Plan Risk Responses is the process of developing options, selecting strategies, and agreeing on actions to address overall project risk exposure, as well as to treat individual project risks. This process follows risk identification and analysis (qualitative and/or quantitative).

The goal is to enhance opportunities and reduce threats to project objectives.

Key Response Planning Concepts

Risk Trigger (Symptom)

A sign or event that provides warning that a risk is about to occur or has already occurred. Triggers are crucial for timely activation of response plans.

Residual Risk

The risk that remains after risk responses have been implemented. It's the "leftover" risk that the project team may decide to accept or monitor.

Secondary Risk

A new risk that arises as a direct result of implementing a risk response. These must also be identified and managed.

Contingency Plan

A planned response ("Plan B") to be performed if a specific risk trigger indicates the risk event has happened or is about to happen.

Fallback Plan

A plan to be used if a primary risk response proves ineffective or if an accepted risk occurs with greater-than-anticipated impact.

Workaround

An unplanned response to a threat that has occurred for which no prior response was planned or the planned response was ineffective. Developed "on the fly."

Contingency Reserve

Funds, time, or resources allocated by the Project Manager for "known-unknowns" (identified risks whose responses are planned via contingency plans).

Management Reserve

Funds, time, or resources allocated by top management for "unknown-unknowns" (unforeseen risks that were not identified).

(Source: Slides: Project Risk Management - Part 1, Slides 52-53; Mock Exam 3, Q1 SA; Mock Exam 2, Q3 SA; Mock Exam 7, Q3 SA; CES665 Final Exam 2022, Q8.MCQ9, Q8.MCQ15)

Chapter 7: Strategies for Negative Risks or Threats

When dealing with negative risks (threats), the project team can choose from several strategies:

Avoid (Avoidance)

This strategy involves changing the project plan to eliminate the threat entirely or protect project objectives from its impact. It's the most radical strategy.

Actions:

  • Shutting down the project entirely (most extreme).
  • Clarifying requirements, obtaining information, improving communication, or acquiring expertise to eliminate the risk (often for early-stage risks).
  • Extending the schedule, changing the project strategy, or reducing scope.

Example: If using a new, unproven technology poses a high risk of failure, avoiding this risk could mean switching to a well-known, proven technology, even if it's less advanced.

(Source: Slides: Project Risk Management - Part 1, Slide 56; Mock Exam 1, MCQ4; Mock Exam 3, MCQ6; Mock Exam 5, MCQ3)

Mitigate (Mitigation)

This strategy aims to reduce the probability of occurrence and/or the impact of a risk to be within acceptable threshold limits. It doesn't eliminate the risk but lessens its severity.

Actions:

  • Taking early action is often more effective than repairing damage later.
  • Adopting less complex processes.
  • Conducting more tests or inspections.
  • Choosing a more stable or reliable supplier.
  • Developing prototypes to reduce scaling-up risks.
  • Designing redundancy into a system (e.g., backup power).

Example: To mitigate the risk of delays due to an inexperienced team member, provide additional training or assign a mentor.

(Source: Slides: Project Risk Management - Part 1, Slides 57-58; Mock Exam 3, Q3 SA; Mock Exam 4, Q1 SA; CES665 Final Exam 2022, Q8.MCQ10)

Transfer (Transference)

This strategy shifts the impact of a threat (and often ownership of its management) to a third party. The risk is not eliminated, but the consequences are borne by another entity. It nearly always involves payment of a risk premium.

Actions:

  • Purchasing insurance.
  • Using performance bonds, warranties, or guarantees.
  • Subcontracting high-risk work (e.g., under a fixed-price contract to transfer cost risk to the seller, or a cost-plus contract may transfer cost risk to the buyer).

Example: Buying insurance for potential damage to construction equipment. If damage occurs, the insurer covers the financial loss.

(Source: Slides: Project Risk Management - Part 1, Slides 59-60; Mock Exam 2, Q1 SA; Mock Exam 4, Q1 SA; Mock Exam 8, MCQ6; CES665 Spring 2019 Exam, Q3, Q6)

Accept (Acceptance) - For Threats

This strategy acknowledges the risk but takes no action to address it unless it occurs. It's adopted when it's not possible or cost-effective to address a specific risk in any other way, or for very low-priority risks.

Types:

  • Passive Acceptance: No action is taken other than documenting the strategy. The team deals with the risk if it occurs.
  • Active Acceptance: Involves establishing a contingency reserve (time, money, or resources) to handle the risk if it materializes. A contingency plan might also be developed.

Example (Passive): A minor risk of a one-day delay due to a common illness of a non-critical team member might be passively accepted.

Example (Active): For a risk of equipment breakdown with a moderate impact, a contingency fund is set aside for quick repairs or rental.

(Source: Slides: Project Risk Management - Part 1, Slides 62-63; CES665 Final Exam 2022, Q4; Mock Exam 3, MCQ2; CES665 Spring 2019 Exam, Q21, Q24)

Chapter 8: Strategies for Positive Risks or Opportunities

When dealing with positive risks (opportunities), the project team can choose from several strategies to increase their likelihood or impact:

Exploit

This strategy is selected for high-impact, high-probability opportunities where the organization wishes to ensure that the opportunity is realized. It seeks to eliminate the uncertainty associated with the upside risk by making the opportunity definitely happen.

Actions: Assigning the organization's most talented resources to the project or using new technologies to reduce cost and duration required to complete the project.

Example: If there's a significant bonus for early project completion, the team might exploit this by allocating extra resources to critical tasks to guarantee finishing ahead of schedule.

(Source: Slides: Project Risk Management - Part 1, Slide 64; Mock Exam 2, MCQ1; CES665 Final Exam 2022, Q5, Q8.MCQ5)

Enhance

This strategy is used to increase the probability and/or the positive impacts of an opportunity. It focuses on identifying and maximizing the key drivers of these positive-impact risks.

Actions: Adding more resources to an activity to finish early (increasing probability of an early finish bonus and its positive impact).

Example: If a new technology could potentially speed up a process, the team might invest in training or pilot programs to enhance the likelihood of successfully implementing it and realizing the time savings.

(Source: Slides: Project Risk Management - Part 1, Slide 65; Mock Exam 3, Q3 SA)

Share

This strategy involves allocating some or all of the ownership of an opportunity to a third party who is best able to capture the opportunity for the benefit of the project. Both parties share the benefits.

Actions: Forming risk-sharing partnerships, teams, special-purpose companies, or joint ventures.

Example: A construction company partners with a specialized tech firm to implement an innovative, cost-saving building technique, sharing the resulting profits or recognition.

(Source: Slides: Project Risk Management - Part 1, Slide 66; Mock Exam 1, MCQ5; Mock Exam 2, MCQ7; CES665 Final Exam 2022, Q8.MCQ5)

Accept (Acceptance) - For Opportunities

This strategy involves being willing to take advantage of an opportunity if it arises, but not actively pursuing it or investing resources to make it happen.

Actions: Documenting the opportunity and monitoring for its potential occurrence. No proactive steps are taken to increase its probability or impact.

Example: An opportunity for a slight reduction in material costs due to market fluctuations might be accepted; the team will take advantage if prices drop but won't actively try to influence the market.

(Source: Slides: Project Risk Management - Part 1, Slide 66)

Exam Focus: Matching Strategies to Scenarios

Be prepared to identify the correct risk response strategy based on a given project scenario. Pay attention to keywords like "eliminate the threat" (Avoid), "reduce probability/impact" (Mitigate), "shift impact to third party" (Transfer), "ensure opportunity happens" (Exploit), "increase probability/impact of opportunity" (Enhance), or "partner for mutual benefit" (Share).
